New York's Side Hustle Economy Just Crossed a Major Threshold

For the first time, more than one in three NYC adults report earning supplemental income from a digital stream — and the nature of those streams is changing fast.

By S.C. Thomas Editor-in-Chief, NY Spotlight Report March 4, 2026 7 min read

Photo credit: NY Spotlight Report • March 4, 2026

A survey released this week by the New York City Economic Development Corporation found that 34 percent of adult city residents now report earning income from at least one source outside their primary employment — up from 24 percent in 2022 and 19 percent in 2019. The data reflects a structural shift that economists have been tracking for years: the integration of digital income-generating tools into the financial lives of working New Yorkers.

The survey polled 4,200 adults across the five boroughs and found significant differences across demographic groups. Workers aged 25 to 34 reported the highest rates of digital income, with 47 percent describing at least one active digital income stream. Workers in professional services reported the highest average supplemental earnings, driven primarily by freelance work in their primary areas of expertise.

What New Yorkers Are Doing

The most common categories of supplemental income: freelance services in one's primary occupation (32 percent), digital content and media including newsletters and YouTube (21 percent), e-commerce including print-on-demand and digital product sales (18 percent), sharing economy services (17 percent), and passive income tools including bandwidth sharing (12 percent).

Growth is concentrated in digital categories. The tools cited most frequently in qualitative interviews: Beehiiv for content creators; Gumroad and Etsy for digital product sellers; Redbubble and Teepublic for print-on-demand designers; EarnApp and Honeygain for bandwidth sharing. The growing adoption of AI writing tools — particularly Jasper and Grammarly — was described by several respondents as the factor making digital content creation time-efficient enough to pursue alongside full-time employment.

The Geographic Distribution

Manhattan respondents reported the highest rates of high-earning supplemental income, consistent with the concentration of finance and professional services workers whose expertise commands high freelance rates. Brooklyn showed the highest rates of e-commerce and digital content income. The Bronx showed the fastest growth in supplemental income participation year-over-year, driven largely by sharing economy and bandwidth-sharing activity — lower-barrier entry points for workers without established professional networks.

The Policy Question

The data raises questions for city policymakers about how to support the growing digital economy. Tax compliance infrastructure for gig and digital income remains inadequate for the volume of participants. The city's Small Business Services division has begun piloting digital literacy programs aimed specifically at helping residents formalize and optimize their supplemental income activities — a recognition that the informal digital economy has grown large enough to warrant institutional attention.
