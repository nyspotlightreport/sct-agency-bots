Inside Amazon KDP's Low-Content Publishing Market: What Sells and What Doesn't

Puzzle books, planners, and journals generate millions in royalties annually. Understanding which niches remain viable is the only question that matters.

By S.C. Thomas Editor-in-Chief, NY Spotlight Report March 8, 2026 10 min read

Photo credit: NY Spotlight Report • March 8, 2026

Amazon's Kindle Direct Publishing platform processes millions of book uploads annually, and a growing proportion are "low-content books" — journals, planners, puzzle books, and logbooks whose value lies not in writing but in structure. For entrepreneurs who understand this market, it represents a genuine royalty income opportunity with no upfront cost, no inventory risk, and no customer service burden.

How the Category Works

Low-content books occupy a specific consumer need: structured blank space for a specific purpose. A fitness log buyer is not looking for fitness advice; they want a well-designed framework for recording workouts. A gratitude journal buyer wants prompts that encourage reflection. The creator's job is to understand the specific structure that serves a specific use case better than competing products.

This framing clarifies the oversaturation question. The concern that "the market is full of journals" misses the specificity of what buyers search for. Amazon searches for "fitness workout log 90 day" and "gratitude journal 365 prompts minimalist" are essentially different markets.

The Profitable Niches in 2026

Niche-specific puzzle books perform better than generic ones because their discoverability is higher relative to competition. Word searches for nurses, large-print Sudoku for seniors, crosswords organized around specific hobbies — these are searchable, specific, and underserved. Budget and finance logbooks tied to specific methodologies — zero-based budgeting logs, envelope-method cash trackers — routinely show meaningful search volume and manageable competition in keyword research using tools like Ahrefs or SEMrush.

The Production Reality

A founder who produces one book per week can build a catalog of fifty-two titles in a year. At average royalties of $1.50 to $3.00 per sale and typical mid-performing sales volumes, a fifty-title catalog represents a plausible $300 to $800 per month in royalties at no ongoing cost.

The Platform Mechanics

Amazon's KDP operates on a 60 percent royalty for paperbacks, minus printing costs. A $7.99 paperback with $2.50 in printing costs generates approximately $2.29 per sale. A book selling 100 copies per month generates $229 per month in perpetuity, for a one-time investment of two to four hours. International sales from markets in the UK, Germany, Japan, and Australia add meaningfully to royalties for titles that gain traction — with no additional action required by the publisher.
