Michelle Zauner's Crying in H Mart: Food Loss and Identity

By S.C. Thomas  |  September 24, 2020  |  NY Spotlight Report

In the midst of a year marked by uncertainty and upheaval, Michelle Zauner's memoir "Crying in H Mart" emerges as a poignant exploration of grief, identity, and the indelible link between culture and cuisine. Published in the form of an essay in The New Yorker earlier this year, Zauner's narrative has already captured significant attention, promising a forthcoming full-length memoir that delves deeper into her personal story. As the vocalist and guitarist of the indie pop band Japanese Breakfast, Zauner's transition from music to memoir writing showcases her versatility and depth as a storyteller.

At the heart of "Crying in H Mart" is the profound and deeply personal experience of losing her mother to cancer, a relationship intricately woven with threads of Korean cuisine and cultural identity. For Zauner, H Mart, a popular Asian grocery chain, becomes a sanctuary of memories and emotions. It is within the aisles of this store that she grapples with the complexities of her own identity as a Korean-American, finding solace and connection through the act of cooking and consuming the foods she once shared with her mother.

Zauner's narrative is a testament to the power of food as a vessel for memory and identity. The specificity with which she describes dishes like kimchi, galbi, and soondubu jjigae transcends the mere act of eating, transforming each meal into a ritual of remembrance and a means of preserving her heritage. Her writing is rich with sensory detail, painting vivid pictures of bustling markets, aromatic kitchens, and the quiet intimacy of family meals. Through these culinary experiences, Zauner navigates the dualities of her existence, straddling the line between her American upbringing and her Korean roots.

Her story resonates on multiple levels, offering not only a personal account of loss but also a broader commentary on the immigrant experience in America. For many first and second-generation immigrants, food is an anchor to their cultural heritage, a tangible connection to a homeland that may be geographically distant yet emotionally near. Zauner captures this beautifully, illustrating how cooking can serve as a form of resistance against the erasure of cultural identity and as an affirmation of one's place within the tapestry of American society.

Moreover, "Crying in H Mart" speaks to the universal experience of grief and the ways in which individuals cope with the absence of a loved one. Zauner does not shy away from the raw and often painful emotions that accompany such a loss. Instead, she confronts them with honesty and vulnerability, inviting readers to reflect on their own relationships with family, culture, and self. Her ability to articulate the complexities of these themes is a testament to her skill as a writer, drawing readers into a narrative that is as heartrending as it is uplifting.

As we anticipate the release of Zauner's full-length memoir, the excerpt that is "Crying in H Mart" stands as a powerful introduction to her voice and story. Her capacity to weave together the strands of food, family, and identity into a cohesive and compelling narrative is a remarkable achievement, setting the stage for what promises to be a deeply moving and insightful exploration of her life.

In a year when many have been forced to confront their own relationships with loss and identity amid a global pandemic, Zauner's story is particularly resonant. It serves as a reminder of the enduring power of storytelling to heal, connect, and inspire, offering comfort in the shared human experience of love, loss, and the search for self. Michelle Zauner's "Crying in H Mart" is not only a tribute to her mother but also a celebration of cultural heritage and the enduring bonds of family, presented through the universal language of food. As readers, we are privileged to accompany her on this journey, savoring each moment of her richly woven tale.
