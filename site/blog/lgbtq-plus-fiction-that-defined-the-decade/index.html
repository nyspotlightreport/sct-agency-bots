LGBTQ Plus Fiction That Defined the Decade

By S.C. Thomas  |  January 10, 2024  |  NY Spotlight Report

As the sun sets on the 2020s, the decade leaves behind an indelible legacy in the world of literature, particularly through the vibrant surge of LGBTQ+ fiction that has carved out an essential space in the literary canon. This era has witnessed a profound transformation in storytelling, where voices that were once marginalized have emerged with power and resonance, reshaping narratives and challenging societal norms. From heart-wrenching tales of identity and belonging to joyous celebrations of love and resilience, these works have not only entertained but also educated and inspired a diverse readership.

One of the most defining works of this decade is Ocean Vuong’s "On Earth We're Briefly Gorgeous," published in 2019. Although it graced bookshelves slightly before the 2020s, its impact reverberated throughout the decade. Vuong's semi-autobiographical novel, written as a letter from a son to his illiterate mother, delves into themes of race, class, and the immigrant experience, interwoven with the tender exploration of queer identity. The novel's poetic prose and raw vulnerability have set a benchmark for LGBTQ+ storytelling, offering readers an intimate glimpse into the complexities of intersectionality.

Following closely in Vuong's literary footsteps, Torrey Peters’ "Detransition, Baby" (2021) emerged as a groundbreaking narrative that boldly tackled themes of gender identity and transformation. With humor and empathy, Peters’ novel examines the intricacies of relationships and the societal constructs surrounding gender, earning it a place as the first novel by a transgender woman to be nominated for the Women's Prize for Fiction. "Detransition, Baby" not only challenged the conventional norms of storytelling but also expanded the landscape for trans narratives within mainstream literature.

In the realm of young adult fiction, Adam Silvera's "They Both Die at the End" (2017) persisted in capturing the hearts of readers throughout the decade. Set in a world where individuals are notified of their death 24 hours in advance, this poignant tale of love and existential reflection achieved renewed popularity, particularly on social media platforms like TikTok. Silvera’s novel, with its universal themes of love, loss, and the urgency of living authentically, has resonated deeply with a new generation of readers seeking validation and representation.

The decade also saw the rise of N.K. Jemisin, whose work, while primarily known for speculative fiction, has consistently interwoven LGBTQ+ themes. Her "Broken Earth" trilogy, completed with "The Stone Sky" in 2017, continued to garner attention into the 2020s. Jemisin’s exploration of power, oppression, and the fluidity of identity in a fantastical world challenged readers to question the boundaries of genre fiction and the role of diverse perspectives within it. Her work not only broadened the scope of speculative fiction but also highlighted the necessity of inclusivity in storytelling.

In the sphere of literary fiction, Brandon Taylor's "Real Life" (2020) emerged as a significant contribution to contemporary LGBTQ+ literature. Following the life of Wallace, a Black queer student navigating the complexities of academia and personal relationships, Taylor’s novel is a masterclass in subtlety and emotional depth. The Booker Prize-shortlisted novel offers a poignant commentary on the intersections of race, sexuality, and mental health, capturing the quiet struggles and triumphs of everyday life.

As the decade progressed, the advent of international voices enriched the tapestry of LGBTQ+ literature. Shokoofeh Azar’s "The Enlightenment of the Greengage Tree" (2017), translated into English by Anonymous, offered a magical realist perspective on the Iranian revolution, intertwined with queer themes and narratives of resistance. This work, though slightly earlier, found a new audience in the 2020s, showcasing the global nature of storytelling and the universality of the search for identity and freedom.

These defining works of LGBTQ+ fiction not only reflect the diverse spectrum of human experience but also advocate for a more inclusive world where every voice is heard and celebrated. As we turn the page to a new decade, the influence of these stories will undoubtedly continue to inspire and challenge future generations of readers and writers alike. The 2020s have reaffirmed that literature is a powerful vessel for change, capable of fostering empathy, understanding, and ultimately, transformation in the hearts and minds of its audience.
